## Honest read: investor vs employer

**Employer path is 5× more tractable from LinkedIn than investor path.** Reasons:

- Angels write $10-50k checks; that's not indie-runway money. VCs need traction you don't have yet (revenue, users).
- Founding PM / Head of Product / Product-Founder-in-Residence roles at seed-Series-A startups exist AND pay real money AND actively look on LinkedIn.
- Your artifact (My Research Lab, myresearchlab.app) is a killer portfolio — most people applying for these roles have a resume + side project; you have a **shipped production SaaS with 60+ ADRs**.
- Even if you'd rather be a founder: **cofounder-market-fit conversations happen through employer-adjacent networks**. Best cofounder matches come from being visible in your target space, not from cold DMs to VCs.

**Investor path realistic angles** (do these in parallel, low effort):
- YC / Antler / Techstars / EU Innovation Council / Sequoia Arc — apply directly, LinkedIn is prep not primary channel
- Angels who back open-science / dev-tools / AI-augmented dev — build a shortlist of 20, engage with their posts, DM after 4-6 weeks of engagement
- **The story angle** — "non-developer ships real SaaS with AI" is a 2026 zeitgeist story. Pitch to Substacks / tech journalists / AI newsletters. A single feature can drive 5-10 investor inbound.

Recommendation: **90% employer signal, 10% investor angle.** Let both be visible in your positioning without forcing a choice.

## Content cadence

**Realistic minimums:**
- 3 posts/week for 8 weeks (24 posts) is when you start seeing measurable network + inbound growth
- 5 posts/week for 12 weeks is when serious opportunities show up
- Under 1 post/week: not worth the effort; unfollow habit sets in

**Best time to post (per LinkedIn algorithm in 2026):**
- Tuesday-Thursday, 8-10am your target audience's time zone
- If targeting US: 8am ET (~2pm CET)
- If targeting EU: 8am CET
- Post 1x/day max; more dilutes reach

**Content mix (every week):**
- 40% build-in-public (screenshots, milestones, what you shipped)
- 25% insight / thought pieces (what you learned, methodology, industry commentary)
- 15% narrative / personal (the journey, why you're doing this, honest failures)
- 10% question / engagement (asking your audience for input)
- 10% direct pitch (looking for X, hiring Y, launching Z)

**Don't do:**
- Motivational quotes / grindset content — deadly on LinkedIn now
- Contest-style engagement bait ("like if you agree, comment your favorite tool")
- Overly polished corporate voice — LinkedIn's 2026 algorithm favors raw / authentic

### Post 4 — The "why replication matters" thought piece

**Type:** Industry commentary

**English:**

> The replication crisis in psychology is fifteen years old.
>
> Papers cite each other. Methods sections describe the study. Preregistrations sit on OSF. And still — when someone actually tries to replicate a published finding, they spend weeks reading, re-implementing in Qualtrics from prose, hunting for stimuli, guessing at randomization logic.
>
> Because the study was never in a portable format. Only the report about it was.
>
> I've been thinking about this for six months while building My Research Lab. Our angle: what if the study itself was the citable artifact — versioned, preregistered, forkable in one click? What if "replicate this" meant literally clone the design into your workspace and adapt it?
>
> Not a solution to the replication crisis. But a piece of infrastructure that makes replication less of a heroic effort and more of a normal step.
>
> Researchers reading this: what's the biggest friction in your replication work today? And what would need to be true for you to publish your next study as a forkable object instead of a PDF?

**Why this works:** big idea + specific insight + question to specific audience.

### Post 5 — Behind-the-scenes: prompting

**Type:** Methodology / AI-augmented builder

**English:**

> How does a non-developer ship 60+ architecture decision records?
>
> I've been asked this a lot. So here's my actual process, minus the polish:
>
> **1. Every decision starts with a prompt like:**
> "I need to add [X feature]. Here's my current architecture: [paste ADR]. Here are the constraints: [list]. Give me three implementation options with tradeoffs. Don't pick yet — I want to see them."
>
> **2. Claude gives me options. I ask why until I understand.**
> Not "which is best" — I want to understand the tradeoffs so I can pick with my product goals in mind. The AI has better breadth; I have better product context.
>
> **3. I make the call and ask Claude to draft an ADR.**
> Then I read it critically. Rewrite the parts that don't match my intent. Ship it.
>
> **4. Every week I re-read prior ADRs to check for drift.**
> The AI is happy to contradict its own past advice. Human continuity is my job.
>
> Not "vibe coding." Product management as the primary skill. The AI is the extra team members I don't have.
>
> Anyone else doing this? What's your workflow?

## Engagement strategy beyond posting

Posts alone won't do it. Do these too:

**Daily (15-20 min total):**
1. Comment thoughtfully on 5 posts from people in your target space (VCs, founders, researchers, designers)
2. Reply to every comment on your own posts within 4 hours (algorithm signal)

**Weekly:**
1. DM 3 people you'd genuinely love to talk to — not a pitch, just "your post about X made me think about Y, would love to compare notes"
2. Ask 1 senior person for a 20-min call. Not for a job — for their perspective on the market you're building in

**Monthly:**
1. Turn one high-performing post into a LinkedIn Newsletter edition (deeper cut)
2. Reach out to 1-2 podcasters or Substackers who cover your space

## Beyond LinkedIn

LinkedIn is a starting engine, not a destination. Real conversations happen elsewhere:

- **X / Twitter** — if you're comfortable, cross-post the same content. AI-augmented builder community is very active there.
- **Substack / your own newsletter** — LinkedIn Newsletter is fine to start; own newsletter (Substack / Beehiiv) gives you audience portability
- **Podcasts** — a 45-min "how I built My Research Lab with AI" episode on 3-5 relevant podcasts (Indie Hackers, Product Hunt Radio, AI-adjacent shows) beats 100 LinkedIn posts for depth
- **Product Hunt launch** — plan for once landing page + docs are live. Wednesdays 9am PT. Do the prep work.
- **Conferences** — SPSP, APS, or Polish psychology conferences (KPS, PSPS) — even attending + networking + a short poster/demo would drive real conversations

## Metrics to track (light — don't over-optimize)

Weekly check:
- Post impressions (LinkedIn shows this)
- Post comments (higher-value than likes)
- Profile views (up-and-to-the-right?)
- DMs received (any qualified inbound?)
- One-off metrics: press mentions, signups from LinkedIn traffic, partnership inquiries

Ignore: like counts, follower count (vanity), engagement rate (industry-benchmarks are meaningless for solo builders)

## Timeline to results (honest)

- **Weeks 1-2:** Learning voice, testing what resonates. Small audience. This is normal.
- **Weeks 3-4:** One post breaks out — 1000+ impressions instead of 100. This is when the algorithm decides you're worth boosting.
- **Weeks 6-8:** Consistent 500-2000 impression posts. First qualified DMs arrive.
- **Weeks 10-12:** First real conversations — investor exploration, employer intro, partnership discussion.
- **Month 4+:** If you kept the cadence, you have real audience momentum + real inbound.

Most people quit at week 3 because nothing measurable happened yet. Don't.
